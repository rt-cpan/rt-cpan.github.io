> On 1/17/12 12:51 PM, Jeffrey Ryan Thalhammer wrote:
> > Does this warrant a Perl::Critic policy? Or does the Perl compiler > > handle this well enough?
> > Interesting question. > > At the basic level of it being handled by the compiler, I don't think > that a policy is necessary. > > Alternatively, I'm sure not many people know about the "+"/";" fixes; > it would be good if more did. A policy could help in spreading that. > But I think that, due to "misinterpretation" by the compiler, many > people will have already changed their code in a way such that the > policy wouldn't be triggered that often.
I'm not sure what kind of policy is being proposed. My first thought was that it is hard to do a lot in this area because PPI's idea of what is a hash constructor and what is a block differs from Perl's. I acquired a commit bit on Adam's repository for some reason a while back, and was able to fix a few of these (curlys after 'return' are documented as being hash constructors, for example) but in the main I am not smart enough to make any improvements. A policy requiring disambiguation could be written, but I'm not sure how comprehensive it should be, and whether it should be in core. On the one extreme, the policy could require all left curlys to be preceded by a unary plus or followed by a semicolon. That's something that is well within the capabilities of PPI. Or, it could be more focused: RJBS, in a reply to my post says that he always writes 'map {; ...}'. That's another one PPI could do easily. I guess the bottom line is that I think a policy that requires disambiguation everywhere (or in some well-defined set of circumstances, such as in a 'map') is certainly possible. But I don't think the tools are up to a "Perl sees this as an expression but you wanted a block" policy. Further, I fear that having in core a policy that requires disambiguation would be more trouble than help. Perl already does a pretty good job of disambiguation. A stand-alone policy might be a good idea. Of course I have said that many times before and never done anything about it. My original intent in writing the Dupin post was to raise awareness of the fact that Perl might not see the curlys the way you intend, and that the error message when that happens might not say what the problem is very clearly. It appears that the post has attracted a lot of attention (more than I ever anticipated, or maybe even intended), but I can't say whether the attention is because people got what I intended out of the post, or whether it's just the novelty of an 1840s detective solving Perl problems.
